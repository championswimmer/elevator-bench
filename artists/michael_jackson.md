# Michael Jackson: A Factual Life History

**Michael Joseph Jackson** (August 29, 1958 – June 25, 2009) was an American singer, songwriter, dancer, and philanthropist. Dubbed the **"King of Pop"**, he is widely regarded as one of the most culturally significant figures of the 20th century and is often called the greatest entertainer of all time. Over a four-decade career, he broke racial barriers in popular music, sold an estimated 500 million records worldwide, and permanently shaped the art forms of music videos, live performance, and popular dance.

---

## Early Life

Michael Joseph Jackson was born on August 29, 1958, in Gary, Indiana — a working-class steel town outside Chicago. He was the eighth of ten children raised by his parents in a modest two-bedroom house on Jackson Street. His mother, **Katherine Jackson** (née Scruse), was a devout Jehovah's Witness who played clarinet, cello, and piano and worked part-time at Sears. His father, **Joe Jackson**, was a former boxer who operated a crane at US Steel and played guitar in a local rhythm and blues band called The Falcons.

Michael grew up alongside five brothers — Jackie, Tito, Jermaine, Marlon, and Randy — and three sisters — Rebbie, La Toya, and Janet. The family's modest income meant nine people sharing cramped quarters, and this closeness would both forge the family bond and fuel the driving ambition that Joe Jackson instilled in his children through relentless rehearsals.

Joe Jackson began training his older sons in music around 1962–1963. He later acknowledged that he regularly administered physical punishment to keep the children disciplined during practices. Michael, as one of the youngest participants, later said the strict environment caused him loneliness and emotional pain: *"My childhood was completely taken from me."* Despite the hardships, the musical education forged in those early years would prove extraordinary.

---

## The Jackson 5

In 1964, six-year-old Michael joined his brothers' act — then called the **Jackson Brothers** — playing congas and tambourine as a backup musician. By 1965, he had taken over as the primary lead vocalist alongside Jermaine, and the group was rechristened **The Jackson 5**. That same year, the group won a local talent contest, with young Michael wowing audiences by dancing to Robert Parker's "Barefootin'" and singing the Temptations' "My Girl."

From 1966 to 1968, the Jackson 5 built their reputation touring the Midwest on the **Chitlin' Circuit** — a network of Black-owned venues — opening for artists such as Sam & Dave, the O'Jays, Gladys Knight, and Etta James. In August 1967, they won the weekly amateur night competition at the legendary **Apollo Theater** in Harlem.

The group recorded their first single, **"Big Boy"**, for the Gary-based Steeltown Records in 1968. Their big break came when Bobby Taylor of Bobby Taylor & the Vancouvers brought them to the attention of **Motown Records** founder Berry Gordy after seeing them perform in Chicago. Gordy was so impressed he signed them immediately. The Jackson family subsequently relocated to Los Angeles.

In 1969, Motown arranged for **Diana Ross** to introduce the Jackson 5 to the public. Their Motown debut single, **"I Want You Back"** (January 1970), shot to number one on the *Billboard* Hot 100 and remained there for four weeks. It was followed by three more consecutive chart-toppers: **"ABC," "The Love You Save,"** and **"I'll Be There"** — an unparalleled debut run. *Rolling Stone* described young Michael as "a prodigy" with "overwhelming musical gifts."

During the early 1970s, Michael simultaneously launched a solo career on Motown, releasing four albums between 1972 and 1975: *Got to Be There* (1972), *Ben* (1972), *Music & Me* (1973), and *Forever, Michael* (1975). The title tracks from his first two solo albums sold well as singles, and "Ben" reached number one on the *Billboard* Hot 100 in 1972. He also popularized the **robot dance** when he performed "Dancing Machine" on *Soul Train* in 1974.

Frustrated by Motown's refusal to allow them creative input and ownership of their music, the Jackson 5 departed the label in **1975**, signing with **Epic Records** and renaming themselves **The Jacksons**. (Jermaine, who had married Berry Gordy's daughter, remained at Motown as a solo artist and was replaced by younger brother Randy.) The Jacksons continued recording and touring, with Michael becoming the group's primary songwriter, penning songs such as "Shake Your Body (Down to the Ground)" (1978) and "Can You Feel It" (1980).

The Jackson 5 were inducted into the **Rock and Roll Hall of Fame** in 1997.

## Solo Career

### *Off the Wall* (1979)

In 1977, Michael Jackson starred as the Scarecrow in the big-budget musical film ***The Wiz*** alongside Diana Ross. Although the film flopped commercially, it introduced him to producer and arranger **Quincy Jones**, who would go on to produce three of Jackson's most important solo albums.

Jackson's fifth solo album and first as a fully independent adult artist, ***Off the Wall*** (1979), produced by Jones and released on Epic Records, was a landmark departure from his Motown sound. The album blended post-disco, funk, and R&B and yielded **four US top-ten singles**, including the number-one hits **"Don't Stop 'Til You Get Enough"** and **"Rock with You."** It sold over 20 million copies worldwide and earned Jackson a Grammy Award for Best Male R&B Vocal Performance. Despite its massive success, Jackson felt the album hadn't received the recognition it deserved — and that ambition fueled his next project.

### *Thriller* (1982)

Released on November 29, 1982, ***Thriller*** became the **best-selling album in recorded history**, with an estimated 70 million copies sold worldwide. It topped the *Billboard* 200 chart for 37 weeks and remained in the top 10 for 80 consecutive weeks — still unmatched records. It was the first album to produce **seven *Billboard* Hot 100 top-ten singles**, including the number-one hits **"Billie Jean"** and **"Beat It."**

The album's 14-minute short film for the title track — directed by John Landis and featuring Jackson dancing with a horde of zombies — **redefined the music video as an art form**. Prior to *Thriller*, MTV had rarely aired videos by Black artists; CBS Records lobbied MTV to play "Billie Jean" and "Beat It," and Jackson's subsequent presence on the channel helped open the door for Black artists more broadly. In December 2009, the *Thriller* video became the only music video selected for preservation in the U.S. **National Film Registry** by the Library of Congress.

At the **26th Annual Grammy Awards** (February 1984), Jackson won **8 Grammy Awards from 12 nominations** — both figures setting records at the time. He won Album of the Year, Record of the Year ("Beat It"), and Best Male Pop, Rock, and R&B Vocal performances, among others.

**March 25, 1983** marked another defining moment: Jackson performed at the NBC television special ***Motown 25: Yesterday, Today, Forever*** before an estimated 47 million viewers. Wearing his iconic rhinestone glove and a black fedora, he debuted the **moonwalk** — a gliding backward-step illusion he had refined from techniques taught to him by street dancer Jeffrey Daniel. Berry Gordy later said he was "mesmerized," and *The New York Times* praised the "perfect timing and technique."

### *Bad* (1987) and the Bad World Tour

Jackson's seventh studio album, ***Bad*** (1987), was the **first album in history to produce five US number-one singles**: "I Just Can't Stop Loving You," "Bad," "The Way You Make Me Feel," "Man in the Mirror," and "Dirty Diana." It debuted at number one in the United States and topped the charts in 25 countries.

The **Bad World Tour** (1987–1989) drew 504,000 people to seven sold-out shows at Wembley Stadium in London — a new Guinness World Record. In Japan alone, it attracted 570,000 attendees across 14 sellout dates. The album sold over 35 million copies worldwide.

In 1985, Jackson co-wrote the charity anthem **"We Are the World"** with Lionel Richie, which raised an estimated $63 million for famine relief in Africa and became one of the best-selling singles of all time. That same year, he purchased the **ATV Music Publishing catalog** — containing publishing rights to nearly 4,000 songs, including most of the Beatles' catalogue — for $47.5 million. In 1995, he merged the ATV catalog with Sony's music publishing division to form **Sony/ATV Music Publishing**, retaining a 50% stake.

### *Dangerous* (1991) and Philanthropic Work

Jackson's eighth studio album, ***Dangerous*** (1991, co-produced with Teddy Riley), was certified eight times platinum in the US and sold 32 million copies worldwide. Its lead single, **"Black or White,"** debuted at number one and spent seven weeks atop the *Billboard* Hot 100. In January 1993, Jackson performed a landmark **Super Bowl XXVII halftime show** in Pasadena that drew 133.4 million viewers — the first Super Bowl halftime to outrate the game itself.

On February 10, 1993, Jackson gave a landmark 90-minute interview to **Oprah Winfrey**, watched by over 90 million viewers — the most-watched television interview in US history to that point. He disclosed his vitiligo diagnosis, discussed his childhood abuse, and denied rumors about skin bleaching and other eccentricities.

In 1992, Jackson founded the **Heal the World Foundation**, which brought underprivileged children to his Neverland Ranch and channeled millions of dollars to children affected by war, poverty, and disease. He was recognized by *Guinness World Records* in 2000 for supporting **39 charities** — more than any other entertainer. Over his lifetime, he is estimated to have donated approximately **$500 million** to charitable causes.

### *HIStory* (1995) and *Invincible* (2001)

The double album ***HIStory: Past, Present and Future, Book I*** (1995) debuted at number one and sold an estimated 20 million copies worldwide. Its single **"You Are Not Alone"** made history as the **first song ever to debut at number one** on the *Billboard* Hot 100.

Jackson's final studio album of original material, ***Invincible*** (2001), cost approximately $30 million to record — making it the **most expensive album ever made** at the time. Despite debuting at number one in 13 countries and eventually selling eight million copies, his relationship with Sony Music had deteriorated: Jackson publicly accused Sony chairman Tommy Mottola of racism and failing to support the album, and Sony declined to renew his contract.

## Final Years and Legacy

### Final Years (2005–2009)

Following his **acquittal on all counts** in the *People v. Jackson* trial on June 13, 2005 — after Santa Barbara authorities had charged him with seven counts of child molestation and two counts of intoxicating a minor (charges he denied) — Jackson became increasingly reclusive and moved briefly to **Bahrain** as a guest of Sheikh Abdullah. His finances had become severely strained: he was involved in dozens of lawsuits and was reportedly on the verge of bankruptcy, with debts eventually reaching nearly **$500 million**.

The main house at his beloved **Neverland Ranch** — the 2,700-acre Santa Ynez Valley estate he had purchased in 1988 for $17 million and transformed into a personal amusement park — was closed as a cost-cutting measure. Jackson never returned to Neverland after his acquittal.

In 2006, Jackson relocated temporarily to **Grouse Lodge** in Ireland, where he worked with producers will.i.am and Rodney Jerkins on material that was never completed. He settled in Las Vegas by late 2006. In 2008, Sony and Jackson released ***Thriller 25***, a 25th-anniversary reissue that returned the album to the charts worldwide.

In **March 2009**, despite widespread speculation about his health and finances, Jackson announced a high-profile comeback: a series of **50 concerts at the O2 Arena** in London, dubbed **"This Is It."** More than one million tickets sold in under two hours, shattering records. Jackson began intensive rehearsals at the Forum and Staples Center in Los Angeles under the direction of choreographer Kenny Ortega.

### Death (June 25, 2009)

On **June 25, 2009** — less than three weeks before the first "This Is It" concert was scheduled to begin — Michael Jackson died of **cardiac arrest** caused by acute **propofol intoxication** at his rented mansion in Holmby Hills, Los Angeles. He was 50 years old.

His personal physician, **Dr. Conrad Murray**, had been administering propofol — an intravenous anesthetic — to help Jackson sleep each night. Murray administered propofol, lorazepam, and midazolam on the night of Jackson's death. Paramedics received a 911 call at 12:21 pm Pacific time; Jackson was pronounced dead at **2:26 pm** at Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center.

The Los Angeles County Coroner ruled Jackson's death a **homicide**. Murray was charged with **involuntary manslaughter**, convicted in November 2011, and sentenced to four years in prison (serving approximately two years). A memorial service held on **July 7, 2009** at the Staples Center in Los Angeles — attended by family, friends, and celebrities — was watched by an estimated **2.5 to 3 billion people** worldwide, making it one of the most-watched televised events in history. Jackson was entombed at Forest Lawn Memorial Park in Glendale, California on September 3, 2009.

### Legacy

Michael Jackson's legacy is vast and multifaceted. He is consistently cited by critics, peers, and industry professionals as the greatest entertainer of all time. His accomplishments include:

- **Music:** Best-selling recording artist in history with 500 million+ records sold. The first artist to sell one million digital downloads in a single week. *Thriller* remains the best-selling album in global history, certified 33× platinum in the US.
- **Music Videos:** Transformed music videos from promotional clips into cinematic short films. The *Thriller* video is the only music video preserved in the US National Film Registry. He broke the color barrier on MTV, helping open the channel to Black artists.
- **Dance:** Popularized the moonwalk, the robot, and the anti-gravity lean. Inducted into the **National Museum of Dance Hall of Fame** in 2010 as the first recording artist so honored.
- **Cultural Impact:** Named the Most Successful Entertainer of All Time by *Guinness World Records*. Named by the National Portrait Gallery (London) as the most depicted cultural figure of the 20th century (2018). Named most famous person in the world by *Guinness World Records* in 2006.
- **Racial Integration:** His success on MTV and in mainstream pop helped dismantle barriers for Black artists and made him, in the words of one CNN commentator, "the Jackie Robinson of MTV."
- **Philanthropy:** Estimated donations of over $500 million across 39 charities — more than any other entertainer in history (*Guinness World Records*, 2000). Honored by Presidents Ronald Reagan, George H.W. Bush (three White House visits total) for humanitarian work.
- **Posthumous Presence:** His estate has earned over $2 billion in ticket revenue from *MJ The Musical* (which premiered on Broadway in 2022 and won the Tony Award for Best Actor in a Musical for Myles Frost), the documentary film *Michael Jackson's This Is It* (2009, which grossed over $260 million worldwide), and two Cirque du Soleil productions. In **February 2024**, Sony Music acquired half of his music catalogue and recording masters for **$600 million** — the largest music acquisition for a single artist in history.
- **Biographical Film:** A biographical film titled *Michael*, directed by Antoine Fuqua and starring Jackson's nephew Jaafar Jackson, is scheduled for release in **April 2026**.

Michael Jackson's music, dance, fashion, and philanthropy continue to influence artists across every genre worldwide. His complex personal life — including battles with vitiligo, painful childhood memories, two child sexual abuse investigations (he was acquitted in 2005 and the 1993 case resulted in no criminal charges), and severe financial difficulties in his final years — ensured that he remained one of the most scrutinized and debated public figures of the modern era. Yet his artistic output stands undiminished: he remains, more than fifteen years after his death, one of the best-known and most beloved entertainers in the history of recorded music.
