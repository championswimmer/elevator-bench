# The Rolling Stones: A Factual Life History

> **Genre:** Rock · Blues rock · R&B · Hard rock · Country rock  
> **Origin:** London, England  
> **Years active:** 1962–present  
> **Core members:** Brian Jones · Mick Jagger · Keith Richards · Bill Wyman · Charlie Watts · Mick Taylor · Ronnie Wood

---

## Early Formation

The story of the Rolling Stones begins not on a stage, but on a railway platform. On **17 October 1961**, on platform two of Dartford station in Kent, **Mick Jagger** (born 26 July 1943) and **Keith Richards** (born 18 December 1943) ran into each other for the first time in years. They had attended Wentworth Primary School together in the early 1950s before losing touch when the Jagger family relocated. What re-ignited the connection was the stack of American blues and R&B records under Jagger's arm — albums by Muddy Waters and Chuck Berry that Richards immediately recognised as the music he, too, had been obsessing over. A musical partnership formed almost immediately.

The two linked up with guitarist **Dick Taylor**, a mutual friend, and began gathering informally to listen to records and play. Eventually a loose group called **Little Boy Blue and the Blue Boys** coalesced around them, with Jagger developing his distinctive voice by mimicking the American blues singers he heard on record.

The crucial third piece arrived in **April 1962** when Jagger and Richards made their way to the **Ealing Jazz Club** in London to hear Alexis Korner's Blues Incorporated. Among the musicians that night was a flamboyant slide guitarist performing as "Elmo Lewis" — in tribute to his hero Elmore James. His real name was **Brian Jones** (born 28 February 1942, Cheltenham), and he was already deeply embedded in the London blues underground. Jones was intent on forming his own blues outfit and placed an ad in *Jazz News* seeking like-minded musicians. Jagger and Richards answered it. A keyboardist named **Ian Stewart** joined, and the core of a new band was taking shape.

On **12 July 1962**, the group — Jones, Jagger, Richards, Stewart, Taylor, and stand-in drummer Mick Avory (later of The Kinks) — debuted as the **Rollin' Stones** at the Marquee Club in London. The name was coined by Jones during a phone call to *Jazz News* when asked for the band's name; his eyes landed on a Muddy Waters LP on the floor, and he saw the track listing — "Rollin' Stone." The spelling was soon formalised to **The Rolling Stones**.

By **December 1962**, Taylor had departed and was replaced by bassist **Bill Wyman** (born 24 October 1936), who sweetened the audition by offering use of his amplifiers to the under-equipped band. In **January 1963**, jazz-scene drummer **Charlie Watts** (born 2 June 1941) — who had already played with Blues Incorporated — stepped in to complete the classic lineup. The first public performance of this settled roster took place on **14 January 1963** at the Flamingo Club in Soho.

In **February 1963**, promoter Giorgio Gomelsky secured the band a Sunday afternoon residency at the **Crawdaddy Club** in Richmond, London. Word spread quickly, with local newspaper coverage and even a visit from **The Beatles**, who dropped by to witness the act generating such excitement in the south London suburbs. The Stones were becoming the house band for a scene hungry for something rawer and less polished than Merseybeat pop.

In **May 1963**, 19-year-old **Andrew Loog Oldham** — who had briefly worked in publicity for the Beatles — pushed Gomelsky aside and signed the Stones to a management contract. He shared the deal with booking agent Eric Easton. Oldham had a clear vision: where Brian Epstein had dressed the Beatles in suits, Oldham would make the Stones the antithesis — threatening, unkempt, and dangerous. Ian Stewart was quietly removed from the official lineup ("He just doesn't look the part," Oldham said), though Stewart continued as road manager and session pianist for decades. Oldham also controversially reduced the band members' stated ages in press releases to make them seem like teenagers. **Decca Records** — which had famously turned down the Beatles — signed the Stones on favourable terms, including three times the standard royalty rate and full artistic control.

The Stones' debut single, a cover of Chuck Berry's **"Come On"**, was released on **7 June 1963**, climbing to No. 21 on the UK Singles Chart. A cover of Lennon-McCartney's "I Wanna Be Your Man" followed, reaching No. 12. By early 1964, Buddy Holly's "Not Fade Away" — given the Bo Diddley treatment — reached No. 3 in the UK, and the band's self-titled debut album arrived in April 1964, charting at No. 1 in the UK. In **1964**, two opinion polls named the Rolling Stones the most popular group in Britain, outranking even the Beatles.

## Rise in the 1960s

### The British Invasion and America

The **first Rolling Stones US tour in June 1964** was, by Bill Wyman's own account, "a disaster." Without an American hit record, the band had little leverage; when they appeared on *The Hollywood Palace* variety show, guest host Dean Martin mocked their hair and performance. But the seeds had been planted. The band spent two days recording at **Chess Studios** in Chicago, meeting Muddy Waters, Chuck Berry, and other heroes whose music had shaped the Stones' entire musical philosophy. Those sessions produced the band's first UK No. 1: a cover of Bobby and Shirley Womack's **"It's All Over Now"**.

By late 1964, the momentum was building on both sides of the Atlantic. The band appeared on *The Ed Sullivan Show* in October; Sullivan reportedly called them "a grubby lot" in private, though he booked them again the following year. Their fifth UK single, a cover of Willie Dixon's **"Little Red Rooster"** (November 1964), became their second UK No. 1.

The breakthrough came in **summer 1965**. During the third North American tour, Keith Richards woke in the middle of the night and instinctively recorded a guitar riff on a cassette player — a chugging, fuzz-toned figure he had been working through in his sleep. When he played it back the following morning, the tape also recorded him snoring afterward. That riff became the opening hook of **"(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction"**, which Richards had intended as a scratch guide for a brass section. The band recorded it with fuzz guitar instead. Released in June 1965, the song became the Rolling Stones' **first US No. 1**, spending four weeks atop the *Billboard* Hot 100 and establishing them as genuine international stars. A second US No. 1, **"Get Off of My Cloud"**, followed in autumn 1965.

### Jagger-Richards: A Great Songwriting Partnership

Manager Andrew Oldham had pushed Jagger and Richards to write original material early, recognising that reliance on covers of "middle-aged blacks" — as he bluntly put it — would limit the band's commercial ceiling. The partnership developed slowly at first, but by 1966 had come fully into its own. **_Aftermath_** (April 1966) was the first Rolling Stones album composed **entirely** of Jagger-Richards originals. It reached No. 1 in the UK and No. 2 in the US, and demonstrated the band's expanded ambition: Brian Jones, whose multi-instrumental talents were at their peak, added sitar to the No. 1 single **"Paint It, Black"**, dulcimer to "Lady Jane", and marimba to "Under My Thumb." The album also contained the nearly 12-minute "Goin' Home", one of the earliest examples of extended jamming on a pop record.

Between 1964 and 1967, the Stones released hit after hit in both the UK and US: **"Play With Fire"**, **"19th Nervous Breakdown"**, **"Paint It, Black"** (No. 1 UK and US), **"Have You Seen Your Mother, Baby, Standing in the Shadow?"**, **"Let's Spend the Night Together"**, and **"Ruby Tuesday"** (No. 1 US, March 1967). The Stones' provocateur image — long unwashed hair, sexually charged performances, confrontational attitudes — set them up as the dark counterpart to the Beatles' clean-cut appeal.

### The Drug Raids and Legal Troubles

In **February 1967**, Sussex police — tipped off by the *News of the World* newspaper — raided a party at Keith Richards' home, **Redlands**. Jagger, Richards, and art dealer Robert Fraser were subsequently charged with drug offences. In June 1967, both Jagger and Richards received prison sentences (three months and twelve months, respectively), which were overturned on appeal within days amid a wave of public outrage. *The Times* ran a famous editorial entitled **"Who Breaks a Butterfly on a Wheel?"**, condemning the severity of the sentencing. Brian Jones, whose own home was raided in May 1967, was separately fined £1,000 and placed on three years' probation after his drug trial in December 1967. Oldham, frightened by the police pressure, departed for America and severed his management relationship with the band. These legal crises reinforced the Stones' image as outlaws — but they also marked the end of the carefree era.

## Turbulence and Reinvention

### Return to Roots and the "Golden Age"

Amid the psychedelic experiments of *Their Satanic Majesties Request* (1967) — widely regarded as the band's weakest effort — the Stones reasserted their identity with a decisive return to blues-based rock. **_Beggars Banquet_** (December 1968), produced by Jimmy Miller, opened with the menacing "Sympathy for the Devil" and also contained "Street Fighting Man," inspired by the Paris riots of May 1968. The album reached No. 3 in the UK and No. 5 in the US and is widely considered the beginning of the band's "Golden Age."

### The Death of Brian Jones

By 1968, Brian Jones — the band's founder, its original leader, and its most mercurial talent — had become a liability. His drug convictions made it impossible for him to obtain a US visa. His attendance at recording sessions became erratic. In a meeting at Jones' house on 8 June 1969, Jagger, Richards, and Watts gently informed Jones he was being replaced. Jones reportedly said: "I've left, and if I want to I can come back."

Less than a month later, on **3 July 1969**, Brian Jones was found dead at the bottom of the swimming pool at his home, **Cotchford Farm** in East Sussex. He was 27. A coroner's inquest returned a verdict of "death by misadventure," citing drug and alcohol intoxication. The circumstances have never been fully resolved and remain a subject of speculation.

The Rolling Stones had already recruited **Mick Taylor** (born 17 January 1948), recommended by John Mayall, as Jones' replacement. Two days after Jones' death — on **5 July 1969** — they performed a tribute concert for him at a free show in **Hyde Park, London**, in front of an estimated **250,000 people**. Jagger read from Shelley's elegy *Adonaïs*, and the band released white butterflies in Jones' memory before opening with a Johnny Winter number. It was also the concert at which the Stones were first introduced by stage manager Sam Cutler as **"The Greatest Rock and Roll Band in the World"** — a billing that stuck.

### Altamont and the End of an Era

The US tour of late 1969, documented in the Maysles brothers' film *Gimme Shelter*, ended catastrophically. At the **Altamont Free Concert** at Altamont Speedway near San Francisco on **6 December 1969**, the Hells Angels motorcycle club had been engaged as security in exchange for beer. During the Stones' set, an 18-year-old fan named **Meredith Hunter** was stabbed and beaten to death by Hells Angels members after they spotted him approaching the stage brandishing a revolver. Three other concert-goers died during the event from other causes. Altamont became a symbol of the dark end of the 1960s idealism — a counterpoint to Woodstock that had occurred just months before.

Despite the shadow cast by Altamont, the Stones' creative output remained extraordinary. **_Let It Bleed_** (December 1969) reached No. 1 in the UK and featured "Gimme Shelter" (with Merry Clayton's searing guest vocals), "Midnight Rambler," and "You Can't Always Get What You Want" — performed with the London Bach Choir. **_Sticky Fingers_** (1971), the first release on their own **Rolling Stones Records** label and featuring an Andy Warhol-designed zipper cover, contained "Brown Sugar" and "Wild Horses."

### *Exile on Main St.* and Life in France

Facing enormous tax liabilities in Britain, the band became **tax exiles** in 1971, relocating to the south of France. Much of 1971 was spent at **Villa Nellcôte** — guitarist Keith Richards' rented 16-room mansion in Villefranche-sur-Mer — where the band set up a makeshift recording studio in the basement. The sessions, conducted amid a near-constant haze of drug use, visitors, and technical chaos, produced what many consider the greatest rock and roll album ever made: **_Exile on Main St._** (May 1972). The sprawling double album — 18 tracks drawing on blues, country, gospel, soul, and rock — was initially received with mixed reviews but has since been reappraised as the pinnacle of the Stones' art. Rolling Stone magazine would later rank it No. 7 on its list of the 500 greatest albums of all time.

### Mick Taylor Departs, Ronnie Wood Arrives

After **_Let It Bleed_**, **_Sticky Fingers_**, and **_Exile on Main St._**, the band continued releasing commercially successful albums — **_Goats Head Soup_** (1973, No. 1 UK and US) and **_It's Only Rock 'n Roll_** (1974, No. 1 US) — though critics grew less enthusiastic. At the end of 1974, **Mick Taylor** resigned, citing underappreciation of his contributions and growing disillusionment with the band's increasingly chaotic working environment.

Several guitarists auditioned for the role, including Peter Frampton, Jeff Beck, and Rory Gallagher. The Stones chose **Ronnie Wood** (born 1 June 1947), a guitarist and bassist then with the Faces, who had already been informally playing and recording with Richards for a year. Wood officially joined for the **1975 Tour of the Americas** — which kicked off with the band performing on a flatbed trailer being towed down Broadway in New York City — and has remained a Stone ever since.

## Later Career

### Commercial Peak and Internal Tensions (1978–1989)

The mid-1970s had been creatively adrift; by 1975 the punk movement was declaring bands like the Rolling Stones obsolete. The Stones' 1976 album **_Black and Blue_** was dismissed by critics and used partly as an extended audition reel. In **Toronto in February 1977**, Richards was arrested by the RCMP after 22 grams of heroin were found in his hotel room, facing a charge of importing narcotics that carried a minimum seven-year sentence. The case dragged on for over a year before Richards received a suspended sentence and was ordered to play two benefit concerts for the Canadian National Institute for the Blind — which he did with a group called the New Barbarians. The crisis galvanised Richards' determination to overcome his heroin addiction.

The creative renaissance came with **_Some Girls_** (1978), partly a response to punk and disco. It reached No. 1 in the US and No. 2 in the UK, producing the disco-inflected hit "Miss You" and the ferocious "Shattered." **_Tattoo You_** (1981), assembled largely from outtakes, became the best-selling album in the Stones' catalogue, reaching No. 1 in the US and producing the anthemic **"Start Me Up"**. The accompanying **1981 American Tour** was the highest-grossing concert tour of that year, with the band now commanding stadium audiences.

Through the mid-1980s, however, a deep personal and professional rift developed between Jagger and Richards. Jagger pursued solo albums and a separate career while Richards — who found Jagger's solo moves a betrayal — was publicly derisive. Both men produced solo records; the band recorded but barely toured. **_Dirty Work_** (1986) was recorded in an atmosphere of open hostility. The truce came at the end of the decade.

**_Steel Wheels_** (1989) marked a full public reconciliation and a commercial comeback. The album and its **Steel Wheels/Urban Jungle Tour** were a global triumph, grossing hundreds of millions of dollars. The Stones were inducted into the **Rock and Roll Hall of Fame** in 1989.

### Into the 21st Century

**Bill Wyman** quietly departed from the Rolling Stones in January 1993, after 30 years. He was never officially replaced; session bassist **Darryl Jones** has accompanied the band on all tours since. The 1994 album **_Voodoo Lounge_** — the band's first as a quartet, and their first for Virgin Records — won a Grammy Award for Best Rock Album. Its accompanying **Voodoo Lounge Tour** (1994–95) grossed over $320 million. The pattern of massive tours supporting new albums continued with **_Bridges to Babylon_** (1997) and the **Bridges to Babylon Tour** (1997–98).

The **A Bigger Bang Tour** (2005–2007), supporting the album of the same name, became the **highest-grossing concert tour in history at that time**, earning approximately $558 million over nearly two years. The Stones' live enterprise was unmatched in the industry: by 2007, four of the top five highest-grossing concert tours of all time were Rolling Stones tours.

In 2016, the band released **_Blue & Lonesome_**, an album of Chicago blues covers paying tribute to the genre that had inspired them 54 years earlier. It debuted at No. 1 in the UK.

### The Loss of Charlie Watts

On **24 August 2021**, **Charlie Watts** — whose immaculate jazz-influenced drumming had anchored the Stones since January 1963 — died in a London hospital, aged 80, weeks after undergoing a medical procedure. His death was felt profoundly throughout the music world. Keith Richards later said that Watts' passing had "jolted" the band into action on the music they had been deferring.

Session drummer **Steve Jordan**, a long-time collaborator of Richards, stepped in. On **20 October 2023**, the Stones released **_Hackney Diamonds_** — their **31st studio album** and first collection of original songs in 18 years. The album featured guest appearances from Lady Gaga, Paul McCartney, and Stevie Wonder. Watts appeared posthumously on two tracks that had been recorded before his death. The album debuted at No. 2 in the US and No. 1 in several European countries, confirming the Stones' enduring commercial vitality.

## Legacy

The Rolling Stones are, by virtually any measure, one of the most influential and commercially successful acts in the history of popular music. *Rolling Stone* magazine ranked them **No. 4 on the "100 Greatest Artists of All Time"**, behind only The Beatles, Bob Dylan, and Elvis Presley. Estimated record sales exceed **250 million copies worldwide**. As the highest-grossing touring act in the world, they have earned over **$2.2 billion in concert revenue** across their career, selling more than **22 million tickets**.

Their influence on rock music is immeasurable. By championing the Chicago blues — Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Robert Johnson, Chuck Berry — to predominantly white audiences in the 1960s, they played a central role in restoring those artists to wider recognition and ensuring the blues tradition flowed directly into rock and roll. The Jagger-Richards songwriting partnership produced some of the most enduring songs in the rock canon: "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction," "Paint It, Black," "Gimme Shelter," "Sympathy for the Devil," "Honky Tonk Women," "Wild Horses," "Brown Sugar," "Miss You," and "Start Me Up," among scores of others.

The iconic **tongue-and-lips logo** — designed by John Pasche in 1970, based on Jagger's suggestion to evoke the Hindu goddess Kali — has been voted the most iconic logo in the history of popular music and remains one of the most recognisable symbols in all of commercial culture.

Their defiant, anti-establishment image — forged by Andrew Loog Oldham's management strategy, the drug raids of 1967, and their provocateur songwriting — helped define the concept of rock-and-roll rebellion and influenced everything from heavy metal to punk. "Sympathy for the Devil," with its references to Satan attending historical atrocities, was directly cited by heavy metal artists as a seminal influence; the Stones' willingness to explore darkness and transgression opened doors that later generations walked through.

On stage, the Rolling Stones reinvented large-scale live performance, pioneering the use of massive stage sets, elaborate lighting rigs, and stadium-scale amplification. They remain one of the greatest live acts in history. At live performances through the 2010s and beyond — even as Jagger approached his eighties — the band's energy on stage continued to astound critics and audiences alike.

Mick Jagger was appointed **Knight Bachelor** in 2003 for services to popular music, though Richards — who once called acceptance of such an honour "inappropriate for a rock and roll band" — expressed disapproval. Charlie Watts received the **Gold Badge of Merit** from BASCA. The band was inducted into the **UK Music Hall of Fame** in 2004.

From a residency at the Crawdaddy Club in 1963 to selling out stadiums more than six decades later, the Rolling Stones have outlasted virtually every musical trend, personal catastrophe, lineup change, and cultural shift that came their way. They have no plans to stop.
