# Led Zeppelin

**Led Zeppelin** were an English rock band formed in London in 1968, comprising vocalist **Robert Plant**, guitarist **Jimmy Page**, bassist-keyboardist **John Paul Jones**, and drummer **John Bonham**. Active for just twelve years, they became one of the best-selling music artists of all time — with an estimated 200 to 300 million records sold worldwide — and are widely cited as one of the most influential bands in the history of rock music. *Rolling Stone* magazine described them as "the heaviest band of all time", "the biggest band of the seventies", and "unquestionably one of the most enduring bands in rock history." In 2025, *Forbes* ranked Led Zeppelin the best rock band of all time.

---

## Formation

Led Zeppelin's origins lie in the ashes of **The Yardbirds**, the celebrated British blues-rock group. In 1966, session guitarist Jimmy Page joined the Yardbirds as a bassist before quickly switching to lead guitar, briefly forming a twin-guitar line-up with Jeff Beck. After Beck's departure in October 1966, Page became the band's sole guitarist. The Yardbirds played their final gig on 7 July 1968 at Luton College of Technology in Bedfordshire, but were still contractually obligated to fulfill a Scandinavian tour. Drummer Jim McCarty and vocalist Keith Relf authorised Page and bassist Chris Dreja to use the Yardbirds name for those dates and to assemble a new line-up.

Page's original vision had been a supergroup involving himself, Jeff Beck, Keith Moon of The Who, and John Entwistle — but that project never materialised. Page's first choice as vocalist was Terry Reid, who declined and instead recommended **Robert Plant**, a young singer from the Midlands then fronting a band called Band of Joy. Plant accepted and in turn recommended his Band of Joy bandmate, drummer **John Bonham**. The final piece fell into place when experienced session musician **John Paul Jones** — already well acquainted with Page from London's studio circuit — contacted Page after hearing about the vacant bass slot.

In **August 1968**, the four played together for the first time in a room beneath a record store on Gerrard Street in London's West End. Page suggested they attempt "Train Kept A-Rollin'", a number the Yardbirds had played. The chemistry was immediate: "As soon as I heard John Bonham play," Jones recalled, "I knew this was going to be great... We locked together as a team immediately."

The band completed the Scandinavian tour as the **New Yardbirds** — their first live performance together took place on 7 September 1968 at Gladsaxe Teen Club in Denmark. Returning to London, they recorded their debut album in just **nine days**, with Page personally financing the sessions. After a cease-and-desist letter from Dreja forced them to relinquish the Yardbirds name, they needed a new one. The name "Led Zeppelin" was inspired by an old quip from Keith Moon and John Entwistle that a supergroup featuring Page and Beck would go down like a "lead balloon." Peter Grant, their manager, dropped the 'a' from *lead* to avoid mispronunciation, and "balloon" was replaced by "zeppelin" — a word that, according to music journalist Keith Shadwick, evoked "the perfect combination of heavy and light, combustibility and grace."

In **November 1968**, manager Peter Grant secured a landmark deal with **Atlantic Records** — a $143,000 advance (approximately $1.3 million today), the largest ever given to a new band at the time. Crucially, the contract granted the band full artistic control over recording, touring schedules, artwork, and promotion. They also retained all publishing rights through their own company, Superhype.

## Rise to Fame

Led Zeppelin's ascent was extraordinarily swift. Their self-titled debut album, **_Led Zeppelin_**, was released in the United States in **January 1969** and reached number 10 on the *Billboard* chart; it peaked at number 6 in the UK on its March release. Featuring tracks like "Good Times Bad Times", "Babe I'm Gonna Leave You", "Dazed and Confused", and the ferocious "Communication Breakdown", the album blended hard rock, blues, and folk in a way that was entirely new. Music critic Steve Erlewine described it as "a significant turning point in the evolution of hard rock and heavy metal."

Within the same year — a feat almost unheard of — Led Zeppelin released their second album. **_Led Zeppelin II_** (October 1969) was recorded largely on the road at various North American studios during their relentless touring. It became their **first number-one album** in both the US and the UK. The album's lead track, "Whole Lotta Love", was released as a single in the US against the band's wishes; it reached number 4 on the *Billboard* chart in January 1970, selling over a million copies. Music scholar Steve Waksman called *Led Zeppelin II* "the musical starting point for heavy metal." Other standouts included "Ramble On" and "Heartbreaker."

In their debut year alone, the band completed four US tours and four UK tours, playing in clubs and ballrooms before rapidly graduating to larger arenas. Some concerts lasted more than four hours, featuring extended and improvised performances. It was during this period that Led Zeppelin — still barely known to mainstream audiences when they first arrived in the US — became a genuine concert phenomenon, driving ticket sales almost entirely through word of mouth rather than radio airplay or television appearances.

In 1970, Page and Plant retreated to **Bron-Yr-Aur**, a remote Welsh cottage, to write the next album. The acoustic and folk-inflected **_Led Zeppelin III_** (October 1970) surprised critics and fans who had expected another heavy assault. It reached number one on both sides of the Atlantic but drew mixed initial reviews for its gentler textures. The opening track, "Immigrant Song", was released as a US single and reached the top twenty despite the band's objections to the format — they fiercely believed in the album as a complete artistic statement and refused to court radio play.

## Peak and Evolution

From 1971 onwards, Led Zeppelin entered what many consider the most celebrated phase of any rock band's career. Their untitled **fourth album** — commonly known as **_Led Zeppelin IV_** — was released on **8 November 1971**. Deliberately stripped of any identifying information on the cover (carrying instead four symbolic runes representing each band member), it was an act of defiance against the music press that had previously accused them of being a "hype." The album contained "Black Dog", "Rock and Roll", "Going to California", and most iconically, **"Stairway to Heaven"** — never released as a single but the most requested and most played song on American rock radio throughout the 1970s. With **37 million copies sold**, *Led Zeppelin IV* is one of the best-selling albums in history. By 2021 it had sold 24 million copies in the United States alone.

**_Houses of the Holy_** followed in **March 1973**, showcasing the band's restless creative evolution. It incorporated synthesisers, mellotron, funk, reggae, and soul alongside their hard rock foundation. Tracks like "The Song Remains the Same", "The Rain Song", and "Over the Hills and Far Away" demonstrated a broadening musical palette. The album topped charts worldwide. The subsequent 1973 North American tour broke concert attendance records: at Tampa Stadium, they played to 56,800 fans, surpassing the record set by The Beatles at Shea Stadium in 1965. Three sold-out shows at Madison Square Garden were filmed for the concert film *The Song Remains the Same* (released 1976).

In 1974, the band took a break from touring and founded their own record label, **Swan Song Records** — named after an unreleased song — through which they would control all future releases. They also signed acts including Bad Company and the Pretty Things to the label.

**_Physical Graffiti_** (February 1975), a sprawling double album and their first release on Swan Song, is widely considered their magnum opus. *Rolling Stone* called it their "bid for artistic respectability", noting that the only bands they had to compete with for "The World's Best Rock Band" title were the Rolling Stones and The Who. Featuring "Trampled Under Foot", the epic "Kashmir", "Ten Years Gone", and "In My Time of Dying", the album was a massive commercial and critical triumph. Upon its release, all of their previous albums simultaneously re-entered the *Billboard* Top 200 — a testament to the depth of their catalogue's appeal. They followed this by playing five sold-out nights at Earls Court Arena in London in May 1975 — at the time the largest arena in Britain.

The band's later-period albums showed both brilliance and strain. **_Presence_** (1976), written and recorded during a forced hiatus after Plant suffered serious injuries in a car crash in Greece, was a more stripped-down, guitar-driven record with a tense, urgent quality. Though it reached number one in the UK and US, it received mixed reviews. **_In Through the Out Door_** (1979), recorded in Stockholm and dominated by John Paul Jones's synthesiser arrangements, reached number one in its second week of release and prompted the entire Led Zeppelin back catalogue to return to the *Billboard* Top 200. In August 1979, they headlined the Knebworth Festival before a crowd of approximately 104,000 — one of their final major concerts.

Throughout this peak era, Led Zeppelin also cemented their reputation for on-the-road excess. Travelling in a private Boeing 720 jet nicknamed *the Starship*, renting out entire floors of hotels, and generating a mythology of rock-star debauchery, they embodied the era of stadium rock. In 1977, they set a new attendance record for a solo indoor concert with 76,229 fans at the Pontiac Silverdome in Michigan — a figure certified by the *Guinness Book of Records* as the largest of its kind to that date.

## Bonham's Death and Disbandment

The 1977 North American tour was ultimately overshadowed by tragedy. On 26 July, while the band was en route to their next concert date, Plant received news that his five-year-old son **Karac had died from a stomach virus** back in England. The remaining tour dates were immediately cancelled, and Led Zeppelin went into an indefinite hiatus amid widespread speculation about their future.

After recovering and recording *In Through the Out Door*, the band planned a major return to North American touring in autumn 1980 — their first US dates since 1977. Rehearsals were set to begin at **Bray Studios** near Windsor. On **24 September 1980**, John Bonham was collected from his home to attend the rehearsals. During the journey he consumed a large quantity of vodka, and continued drinking heavily at the studios. When the rehearsals broke up late that evening, the band retired to Jimmy Page's country home — the Old Mill House in Clewer, Windsor. Bonham fell asleep and was placed in bed on his side.

At 1:45 pm the following day, 25 September 1980, tour manager Benji LeFevre and John Paul Jones found **Bonham dead in his bed**. The cause of death was **asphyxiation from vomit** following the ingestion of an extreme amount of alcohol — an inquest returned a verdict of accidental death. He was 32 years old. An autopsy found no other recreational drugs in his system. Bonham's remains were cremated, and his ashes interred at Rushock parish church in Worcestershire on 12 October 1980.

The planned North American tour was immediately cancelled. Despite rumours that various other drummers might step in, the remaining three members decided that continuing was impossible. On **4 December 1980**, they issued a formal press statement:

> *"We wish it to be known that the loss of our dear friend, and the deep sense of undivided harmony felt by ourselves and our manager, have led us to decide that we could not continue as we were."*

The statement was signed simply: **"Led Zeppelin."**

## Legacy

Led Zeppelin's impact on rock music is immeasurable. They are widely credited, alongside Black Sabbath and Deep Purple, as one of the founding architects of **hard rock and heavy metal**, though the band members themselves often resisted such labelling. Their influence on **album-oriented rock (AOR) and stadium rock** permanently changed the economics and presentation of the music business. In 1988, Geffen Records A&R executive John Kalodner said: "Next to the Beatles they're the most influential band in history... They set the standards for the AOR-radio format with 'Stairway to Heaven'... They're the ones who did the first real big arena concert shows."

Their shadow falls across virtually every major rock movement of the subsequent five decades. Direct musical heirs include **Aerosmith**, **Rush**, **Queen**, **Heart**, **Soundgarden**, **Pearl Jam**, **Nirvana**, and **Smashing Pumpkins**, all of whom have explicitly cited Led Zeppelin as a foundational influence. Even artists from entirely different genres — including **Madonna**, **Lady Gaga**, and **Shakira** — have acknowledged their debt.

In terms of **commercial achievement**, Led Zeppelin hold extraordinary records:
- Eight consecutive UK number-one albums (a record shared with ABBA)
- Six number-one albums on the US *Billboard* 200
- Five albums certified Diamond in the United States by the RIAA
- Over 111.5 million certified sales in the US alone
- Third-highest-selling band in US history, according to RIAA

**Honours and awards** accumulated throughout the following decades:
- **Rock and Roll Hall of Fame**, inducted in **1995** (with the Rock Hall's biography stating they were "as influential" in the 1970s as The Beatles were in the 1960s)
- **Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award**, 2005
- Four recordings inducted into the **Grammy Hall of Fame**
- **UK Music Hall of Fame**, 2006
- **Kennedy Center Honors**, 2012 (presented by President Barack Obama)
- **Polar Music Prize**, 2006
- **VH1's #1 Greatest Artist of Hard Rock**
- Ranked #1 best rock band of all time by *Forbes* magazine, 2025

**Post-disbandment reunions** were sporadic and often fraught. A poorly received appearance at Live Aid in 1985 and an equally troubled set at the Atlantic Records 40th Anniversary concert in 1988 (with Jason Bonham, John's son, on drums) dented their reputation for live performance. The 1995 Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction ceremony produced a more spirited, if tense, performance. The most celebrated reunion came on **10 December 2007** at London's O2 Arena — the **Ahmet Ertegun Tribute Concert** — again with Jason Bonham on drums. Demand was staggering: 20 million online ticket requests were submitted for the approximately 20,000-seat show, earning a *Guinness World Records* citation. Critics hailed it as a triumphant vindication. The concert was later released as the film and live album *Celebration Day* (2012).

In February 2025, the authorised biographical documentary **_Becoming Led Zeppelin_** premiered in IMAX theatres worldwide — the first time all band members had agreed to participate in a full biographical film.

Robert Plant's wailing, operatic voice helped establish the archetype of the "rock god" vocalist; a 2011 *Rolling Stone* readers' poll named him the **Best Lead Singer of All Time**. Jimmy Page's guitar work — blending blues, folk, Eastern scales, and innovative studio techniques such as the violin bow — remains among the most studied and imitated in rock history. John Paul Jones was the consummate musical architect, providing melodic bass lines, orchestral keyboard arrangements, and quiet structural genius. And John Bonham's thunderous, groove-locked drumming — characterised by power, speed, and an unmatched feel for dynamics — is universally considered one of the greatest drum performances ever committed to record.

As *Rolling Stone* wrote: Led Zeppelin were "talented, complex, grasping, beautiful and dangerous — and made one of the most enduring bodies of composition and performance in twentieth-century music."
