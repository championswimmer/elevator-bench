# Frank Sinatra: A Factual Life History

> **Born:** December 12, 1915 — Hoboken, New Jersey, U.S.  
> **Died:** May 14, 1998 — Los Angeles, California, U.S. (aged 82)  
> **Nicknames:** "Ol' Blue Eyes" · "The Chairman of the Board" · "The Voice"  
> **Genres:** Traditional Pop · Jazz · Swing · Big Band · Easy Listening  
> **Labels:** RCA Victor · Columbia · Capitol · Reprise  
> **Active:** 1935–1995

---

## Early Life

Francis Albert Sinatra was born on **December 12, 1915**, at 415 Monroe Street in a tenement in **Hoboken, New Jersey**, the only child of Italian immigrants. His father, **Antonino "Marty" Sinatra**, was a former bantamweight boxer who later rose to the rank of captain in the Hoboken Fire Department. His mother, **Natalina "Dolly" Garaventa Sinatra**, was a formidable personality who became a local power broker in Hoboken's Democratic Party circles.

Sinatra's birth was difficult — he weighed 13.5 pounds and had to be delivered with forceps, leaving permanent scarring on his left cheek and neck and permanent damage to his left eardrum. He was raised in the **Catholic Church** and baptised on April 2, 1916. Throughout his childhood he was afflicted with cystic acne, and his excessively thin frame made him the subject of many jokes later in life — though his mother ensured he was always impeccably dressed, earning him the nickname "the best-dressed kid in the neighborhood."

Sinatra's mother was widely regarded by biographers as the dominant shaping force in her son's personality and ambition. His father, who was illiterate, pushed Frank toward becoming a civil engineer and hoped he would enrol at Hoboken's Stevens Institute of Technology. Sinatra had other ideas. He attended A. J. Demarest High School (later renamed Hoboken High School) but was expelled after attending just **47 days**, reportedly for "general rowdiness," and never graduated.

From an early age Sinatra was drawn to music — particularly **big band jazz** — and idolised **Bing Crosby**. For his 15th birthday, an uncle gave him a ukulele, which he played at family gatherings. He began performing in local Hoboken social clubs and singing for free on radio stations such as **WAAT** in Jersey City. He worked odd jobs — as a delivery boy at the *Jersey Observer* and as a riveter at the Tietjen and Lang shipyard — while dreaming of a career on stage.

His big break came in **1935**, when his mother persuaded a local vocal group called the **3 Flashes** to let Frank join them for an audition on the *Major Bowes Amateur Hour* radio programme. Renamed the **Hoboken Four**, the group won first prize — a six-month touring contract — and Sinatra quickly became the lead attraction. Girls clamoured for him at every stop, much to the jealousy of his bandmates.

---

## Big Band Years

After touring with the Hoboken Four, Sinatra continued to hone his craft performing on New Jersey radio. In **June 1939**, trumpet bandleader **Harry James** — who had heard Sinatra on a radio broadcast — signed him to a two-year contract at $75 a week. Sinatra recorded his first commercial single with James, *"From the Bottom of My Heart,"* but sales were modest. A subsequent recording of *"All or Nothing at All"* also flopped initially, though it would later become a major hit when re-released.

Sinatra grew restless with the James band, hungry for greater stardom. In **November 1939**, he departed to join the far more prominent **Tommy Dorsey Orchestra**, replacing departing vocalist Jack Leonard at $125 a week. It was with Dorsey that Sinatra's career truly ignited. He made his first public appearance with the band on **January 26, 1940**, at the Coronado Theatre in Rockford, Illinois, opening with *"Stardust."* Dorsey recalled: *"You could almost feel the excitement coming up out of the crowds when the kid stood up to sing."*

In his first year with Dorsey, Sinatra recorded over **40 songs**. His first vocal chart hit was *"Polka Dots and Moonbeams"* (April 1940). His fourth charted single, *"I'll Never Smile Again,"* topped the newly-published *Billboard* chart for **twelve consecutive weeks** beginning in mid-July 1940. A string of further hits followed, including *"Everything Happens to Me,"* *"This Love of Mine,"* and *"There Are Such Things."*

Dorsey became a profound influence and something of a **father figure** to Sinatra, who adopted his bandleader's traits of perfectionism and demanding professionalism. Sinatra famously said, *"The only two people I've ever been afraid of are my mother and Tommy Dorsey."* By **May 1941**, Sinatra had topped the male singer polls in both *Billboard* and *DownBeat* magazines.

Yearning to compete with Bing Crosby as a solo artist, and emboldened by a private session of solo recordings in January 1942 that revealed the full power of his voice, Sinatra pushed to leave the Dorsey band. He was hampered by a contract that gave Dorsey **43% of Sinatra's lifetime earnings**, leading to a contentious legal battle finally settled in August 1942. On **September 3, 1942**, Dorsey bade farewell to Sinatra — reportedly telling him, *"I hope you fall on your ass"* — and Sinatra struck out on his own.

---

## Solo Stardom

On **December 30, 1942**, Sinatra made his solo debut at the **Paramount Theatre** in New York City. The reaction was unprecedented. The venue's capacity crowd of bobby soxers — teenage female fans who wore ankle socks — screamed so loudly that the building seemed to shake. Jack Benny, who preceded him on the bill, said, *"I thought the goddamned building was going to cave in. I never heard such a commotion … All this for a fellow I never heard of."* The phenomenon became known as **"Sinatramania."**

Sinatra signed with **Columbia Records** as a solo artist on **June 1, 1943**, and became the idol of a generation. His re-released recording of *"All or Nothing at All"* shot to No. 2. He performed on the *Your Hit Parade* radio programme from February 1943 through December 1944. When he returned to the Paramount in October 1944, only 250 patrons left the first show while **35,000 fans** outside caused a near-riot — the **Columbus Day Riot** — after being denied entry.

Sinatra released his debut album, ***The Voice of Frank Sinatra***, in **1946**, one of the first long-playing records ever marketed to the public. He also expanded into film, appearing in *Anchors Aweigh* (1945) alongside Gene Kelly and earning an Honorary Academy Award for a short film promoting racial and religious tolerance.

However, by the late 1940s and early 1950s, Sinatra's career entered a **steep decline**. His personal life was in turmoil — he left his wife Nancy Barbato for actress **Ava Gardner** (whom he married in 1951), and the resulting tabloid scandal damaged his image. His voice suffered from a vocal cord haemorrhage in 1950. Columbia Records dropped him. His television show was cancelled. His agency, MCA, terminated his contract. At his lowest ebb, he attempted suicide.

The turnaround came in **1953**, when Sinatra lobbied fiercely — and at a drastically reduced fee — for the role of Maggio in the film adaptation of James Jones's novel *From Here to Eternity*. His electrifying performance earned him the **Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor**, reigniting public interest and setting the stage for one of the most extraordinary comebacks in entertainment history.

Sinatra signed with **Capitol Records** in **1953** and was paired with arranger and conductor **Nelson Riddle**. The partnership produced a golden run of albums that redefined popular music as an art form. *In the Wee Small Hours* (1955) — widely considered one of the greatest albums ever recorded — was a landmark "concept album" of melancholic ballads about lost love. *Songs for Swingin' Lovers!* (1956) showcased a jubilant, swinging Sinatra at his most exuberant. The albums *Come Fly with Me* (1958) and *Only the Lonely* (1958) further cemented his reputation as both a supreme balladeer and a master of the up-tempo swing number.

---

## Film and Rat Pack Era

Sinatra's Oscar-winning comeback launched a prolific second chapter as a **major Hollywood star**. He received a Golden Globe for *Pal Joey* (1957), appeared in the musical ensemble *High Society* (1956) alongside Bing Crosby and Grace Kelly, and delivered one of his finest dramatic performances in *The Man with the Golden Arm* (1955), playing a jazz drummer battling heroin addiction — a role that earned him an Academy Award nomination for Best Actor.

His most critically acclaimed film role came with *The Manchurian Candidate* (1962), a political thriller in which he played a Korean War veteran fighting to prevent a Communist-orchestrated assassination — still regarded as one of the finest American films of its era.

Around **Las Vegas** — his adopted playground — Sinatra assembled the **Rat Pack**, a loose confederation of entertainers who performed together and socialised with a legendary joie de vivre. The core group comprised Sinatra, **Dean Martin**, **Sammy Davis Jr.**, **Peter Lawford**, and **Joey Bishop**. They performed residencies at the **Sands Hotel** and became symbols of cool, sophisticated fun. Their camaraderie was put on screen in *Ocean's 11* (1960), a heist comedy filmed simultaneously with their live Sands shows and among the most commercially successful films of the year.

The Rat Pack's social circle reached into the highest levels of American politics. Peter Lawford was the brother-in-law of **John F. Kennedy**, and Sinatra campaigned ardently for Kennedy in the 1960 presidential election, performing at his inaugural gala in January 1961.

In **1960**, Sinatra left Capitol Records to found his own label, **Reprise Records**, asserting artistic independence at a time when few artists had such control. The label signed many of his Rat Pack associates and produced landmark recordings including *Sinatra at the Sands* (1966) — a live album recorded with the **Count Basie Orchestra** and arranger Quincy Jones that many regard as his finest live document. He also recorded celebrated duet albums with **Antonio Carlos Jobim** (*Francis Albert Sinatra & Antonio Carlos Jobim*, 1967) and **Duke Ellington** (*Francis A. & Edward K.*, 1968).

The song *"My Way"* — recorded in **1969** with lyrics adapted from the French chanson *"Comme d'habitude"* — became his signature anthem, an assertion of individualism and resilience that seemed to encapsulate his entire life story. *"New York, New York"* (recorded 1980) became another definitive Sinatra standard, adopted as the unofficial anthem of New York City.

In **1971**, Sinatra announced his **retirement**, citing exhaustion and a desire for privacy. He delivered what he called his farewell concert at the Los Angeles Music Center in June of that year.

---

## Final Years and Legacy

Sinatra's retirement lasted barely two years. By **1973** he was back in the studio, releasing *Ol' Blue Eyes Is Back* and returning to the concert stage with undiminished power. He remained a major touring and recording artist through the 1980s, releasing the triple album *Trilogy: Past Present Future* (1980) and *L.A. Is My Lady* (1984).

In **1983**, Sinatra was honoured at the **Kennedy Center Honors**. In **1985**, President Ronald Reagan awarded him the **Presidential Medal of Freedom** — the highest civilian honour in the United States. In **1994**, he received the **Congressional Gold Medal**, and that same year gave what many would consider one of his last major live performances in Japan.

Sinatra's final studio recording sessions took place in **1994**, for the two-volume *Duets* albums (*Duets*, 1993; *Duets II*, 1994), in which he performed his classic songs alongside contemporary artists including Barbra Streisand, Bono, Natalie Cole, and Tony Bennett. The albums were enormous commercial successes, introducing Sinatra to a new generation of listeners.

His **last public performance** was at the close of the Frank Sinatra Desert Classic golf tournament in **February 1995** at the Palm Desert Marriott Ballroom, where he sang six songs before closing with *"The Best Is Yet to Come."*

In his final years, Sinatra's health declined significantly. He suffered from high blood pressure, bladder cancer, pneumonia, and dementia. He had a first heart attack in January 1997, after which he largely withdrew from public life. On **May 14, 1998**, Sinatra suffered a second fatal heart attack and was rushed to **Cedars-Sinai Medical Center** in Los Angeles. His final words, spoken to his manager Tony Oppedisano, were reported as: *"I'm losing."* He was pronounced dead at **10:50 p.m.** His fourth wife, Barbara Blakely Marx, was by his side.

Frank Sinatra was buried at **Desert Memorial Park** in Cathedral City, California. His original gravestone bore the epitaph *"The Best Is Yet to Come"* and *"Beloved Husband & Father."*

### Legacy

Frank Sinatra is considered one of the **greatest entertainers of the twentieth century** and one of the best-selling music artists of all time, with an estimated **150 million records sold** worldwide. Over his six-decade career he earned **11 Grammy Awards** — including the Grammy Trustees Award, the Grammy Legend Award, and the Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award — as well as an Academy Award, a Golden Globe, the Presidential Medal of Freedom, and the Congressional Gold Medal.

As a recording artist, Sinatra's contribution was transformative. At Capitol Records in the 1950s, working with arrangers Nelson Riddle, Billy May, and Gordon Jenkins, he elevated the popular song album to a serious art form. His mastery of **breath control** — inspired by watching Tommy Dorsey play the trombone — and his interpretive intelligence in communicating the emotional core of a lyric set a standard that influenced virtually every subsequent popular vocalist. American music critic **Robert Christgau** called him *"the greatest singer of the 20th century."*

As an actor, he brought raw conviction to dramatic roles and helped dismantle racial barriers by insisting on integrated audiences and casts at a time when segregation was still routine in Las Vegas and across the American South.

Bono of U2 captured his cultural weight simply: *"Frank Sinatra was the 20th century. He was modern, he was complex, he had swing, and he had attitude. We won't see his like again."*
