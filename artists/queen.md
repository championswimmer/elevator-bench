# Queen

Queen are a British rock band widely regarded as one of the greatest and most influential acts in the history of popular music. Formed in London in 1970, the band's classic line-up comprised **Freddie Mercury** (lead vocals and piano), **Brian May** (lead guitar and vocals), **Roger Taylor** (drums and vocals), and **John Deacon** (bass guitar). Over a career spanning two decades, Queen blended hard rock, glam rock, progressive rock, opera, and pop into a singular sound, producing a catalogue of anthems that continue to resonate across generations.

---

## Formation

The origins of Queen trace back to the mid-1960s and the parallel musical journeys of Brian May and Roger Taylor.

**Brian May** was born on 19 July 1947 near Twickenham, Middlesex. A gifted student at Hampton Grammar School, he and classmate Tim Staffell formed a blues-rock outfit called **1984** (named after the George Orwell novel), which was accomplished enough to share a bill with Jimi Hendrix in 1967. May studied Physics at Imperial College London, but his musical ambitions prevailed. In 1968, he and Staffell formed a new, more experimental group called **Smile**, recruiting **Roger Taylor** — born 26 July 1949 in King's Lynn, Norfolk — as drummer. Taylor had previously fronted a popular Cornwall band called The Reaction, and had moved to London to study dentistry (later switching to biology, earning a BSc from East London Polytechnic).

Meanwhile, **Farrokh Bulsara** — the man the world would know as **Freddie Mercury** — was born on 5 September 1946 in the British Protectorate of Zanzibar. He began piano lessons at age seven and formed a school band, The Hectics, at age twelve while attending St. Peter's School near Bombay, India. His family fled the violence of the 1964 Zanzibar Revolution and resettled in west London, where Freddie enrolled at Ealing College of Art to study graphic design. It was there that he befriended Staffell, eventually becoming a fixture in the Smile entourage — attending shows, critiquing performances, and lobbying to become the band's lead singer.

When Staffell departed Smile in early 1970, Mercury was the obvious choice as replacement. He also coined the group's new name — **Queen** — a reflection of his androgynous persona and grand ambitions. He adopted the surname Mercury, inspired by his own lyric "Mother Mercury, look what they've done to me" from the song "My Fairy King." Queen played their first formal gig on 27 June 1970 at a Cornwall Red Cross benefit.

The final piece of the classic line-up arrived after a chance encounter at a London disco in early 1971. **John Deacon**, born 19 August 1951 in Leicester, was an electronics student at Chelsea College with a background in bands such as The Opposition. Quiet and technically skilled, he complemented the outsize personalities of his bandmates. After several auditions, Deacon was confirmed as bassist, and Queen played their first full-lineup concert on 2 July 1971 at Surrey College.

---

## Breakthrough

Queen spent much of 1972 recording their debut in the off-hours at De Lane Lea Studios — trading studio time in exchange for letting engineers test acoustics — which caught the attention of producer Roy Thomas Baker, who would go on to produce their first five albums. A deal was struck with Trident/Neptune Productions and, in April 1973, EMI Records. Their self-titled debut album was released in 1973, with the single "Keep Yourself Alive" earning a gold certification in both the UK and the US despite modest chart performance.

Their second album, **Queen II** (1974), peaked at number five in the UK. Its single "Seven Seas of Rhye" became the band's first UK Top 10 hit, and stylistically it marked the emergence of the group's signature layered guitars and operatic vocal harmonies. **Sheer Heart Attack** followed later the same year, reaching number two in the UK and earning gold certification in the US. Its standout single **"Killer Queen"** — a vaudeville-inflected pop gem — hit number two in Britain and number 12 on the US Billboard Hot 100, announcing Queen to a global audience.

The true breakthrough came in 1975. After negotiating out of their restrictive Trident contract, and freed by new manager John Reid's simple directive — *"I'll take care of the business; you make the best record you can"* — the band spent a then-extraordinary sum (approximately £343,000 in 2022 terms) recording their fourth album, **_A Night at the Opera_**. The album was ambitious and eclectic, opening with the savage "Death on Two Legs" and culminating in their masterpiece: **"Bohemian Rhapsody."**

A six-minute operatic-rock epic featuring nearly 200 vocal and instrumental overdubs, "Bohemian Rhapsody" was deemed uncommercially long by EMI, but the band held firm. After DJ Kenny Everett played a promotional copy on Capitol London Radio over a single weekend to overwhelming listener response, the label relented. The single spent nine weeks at number one on the UK charts, making it the third-best-selling single in UK chart history and the only single ever to sell over one million copies on two separate occasions (the first in 1975–76, and again following its inclusion in the 1992 film *Wayne's World*). The innovative promotional film shot to accompany it is widely recognised as a forerunner of the modern music video.

---

## Global Stardom

Building on the success of *A Night at the Opera*, Queen released **_A Day at the Races_** in 1976, which hit number one in both the UK and Japan, driven by the gospel-flavoured anthem **"Somebody to Love."** That same year, they drew a crowd of 150,000 to a free concert in London's Hyde Park. In 1977, their world tour played to sold-out arenas, including multiple nights at New York's Madison Square Garden — earning them the MSG Gold Ticket Award for surpassing 100,000 lifetime tickets sold at the venue.

**_News of the World_** (1977) gave the world two of rock's defining anthems: **"We Will Rock You"** and **"We Are the Champions,"** which became the de facto victory songs of sporting events worldwide. **_The Game_** (1980) became their sixth platinum album, topping the Billboard 200 in the US for five weeks, and yielded two US number-one singles: **"Crazy Little Thing Called Love"** and **"Another One Bites the Dust."** It was also the first Queen album to feature a synthesizer. On their subsequent world tour, Queen became the first rock band to headline large-scale stadium shows in South America, playing to a record crowd of over 300,000 in Buenos Aires.

In 1981, a spontaneous studio visit from **David Bowie** led to the recording of **"Under Pressure,"** a collaborative single that reached number one in the UK. Later that year, Queen released their first **Greatest Hits** compilation, which became the best-selling album in UK chart history, ultimately spending close to 1,000 weeks on the UK Albums Chart.

The pinnacle of Queen's live legacy came on **13 July 1985** at **Live Aid**, the Bob Geldof and Midge Ure-organised benefit concert at Wembley Stadium for Ethiopian famine relief — broadcast to an estimated 1.9 billion viewers across 130 countries. Queen, who had rehearsed meticulously for a week at the Shaw Theatre near King's Cross, took the stage at 6:41 pm for a 21-minute set that opened with a spine-tingling truncated "Bohemian Rhapsody" before exploding through "Radio Ga Ga," "Hammer to Fall," "Crazy Little Thing Called Love," "We Will Rock You," and "We Are the Champions." Freddie Mercury's improvisational call-and-response with the 72,000-strong crowd — a wordless "ay-oh" exchange that became known as *"the note heard around the world"* — was an electrifying moment of pure showmanship.

The verdict was unanimous. Bob Geldof declared: *"Queen were absolutely the best band of the day. They played the best, had the best sound, used their time to the full… It was the perfect stage for Freddie: the whole world."* Elton John reportedly ran into Mercury's trailer after the set exclaiming, *"You bastards, you stole the show."* Dave Grohl of Foo Fighters later said: *"Queen smoked 'em. They just took everybody… that's why they should be recognized as one of the greatest rock bands of all time."*

In January 1985, ahead of Live Aid, Queen had already played two nights at the inaugural **Rock in Rio** festival before audiences of over 300,000 per night. Their 1986 world tour in support of **_A Kind of Magic_** — which sold six million copies — concluded on 9 August 1986 at **Knebworth Park** before a crowd of 120,000 fans. It was the last time Queen would perform live with Freddie Mercury.

---

## Freddie Mercury's Final Years

By 1986, rumours had begun to circulate that Freddie Mercury was receiving treatment for HIV. Mercury kept his diagnosis fiercely private, denying his status to the press and confiding only in his closest family and friends. His lifelong companion **Mary Austin** — to whom he referred as his common-law wife and to whom he ultimately bequeathed the bulk of his estate — was among those he trusted.

Despite his deteriorating health, Mercury refused to stop working. Queen recorded two more studio albums in this period: **_The Miracle_** (1989) and **_Innuendo_** (1991), the title track of which reached number one in the UK. Throughout these sessions, Mercury pushed himself relentlessly. Brian May later recalled: *"He just kept saying, 'Write me more. Write me stuff. I want to just sing this and do it, and when I am gone you can finish it off.' He had no fear, really."*

By 1987, Mercury's increasingly gaunt appearance and sporadic cancellations fuelled widespread speculation about his health. His final on-screen appearance came in May 1991, in the music video for **"These Are the Days of Our Lives,"** in which a visibly frail Mercury looked directly into the camera for the closing moments.

On **23 November 1991**, Mercury issued a public statement confirming what the world had suspected: *"I wish to confirm that I have been tested HIV positive and have AIDS. I felt it correct to keep this information private to date in order to protect the privacy of those around me. However, the time has come now for my friends and fans around the world to know the truth and I hope that everyone will join with my doctors and all those worldwide in the fight against this terrible disease."*

**Freddie Mercury died the following day, 24 November 1991, aged 45**, from bronchial pneumonia brought on by complications of AIDS. He is buried in a private location, though a memorial stone stands at the Garden of Remembrance at Kensal Green Cemetery in London.

## Legacy

Queen's impact on music and popular culture has proved enduring and boundless.

On **20 April 1992**, a **Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert** was held at Wembley Stadium before an audience of over 70,000 and a global television and radio audience of up to one billion people. Performers including David Bowie, Elton John, George Michael, Annie Lennox, Metallica, and Guns N' Roses paid tribute to Mercury, and the event raised awareness and funds for HIV/AIDS research. The proceeds helped establish **The Mercury Phoenix Trust**, which has since distributed millions of pounds to AIDS charities worldwide.

In November 1996, a **three-metre bronze statue of Mercury** — sculpted by Irena Sedlecká and depicting him in his iconic fist-raised pose in his yellow military jacket — was unveiled on the shores of Lake Geneva in Montreux, Switzerland, where the band had recorded many of their later albums at Mountain Studios.

John Deacon retired from music following a 1997 one-off performance with the band and Elton John, recording one final song, "No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)," as a tribute to Mercury. A posthumous album, **_Made in Heaven_** (1995), compiled Mercury's studio contributions and debuted at number one in the UK.

In May 2002, the musical **_We Will Rock You_**, written by Ben Elton in collaboration with May and Taylor, opened at London's Dominion Theatre and ran for 12 years, becoming one of the longest-running shows in West End history.

May and Taylor have continued to tour as **Queen + Adam Lambert** (following earlier shows with Paul Rodgers), maintaining the band's global live presence, opening the **91st Academy Awards** in 2019, and receiving Japan's Gold Disc Award — awarded to the most popular Western act in the country — four times between 2005 and 2021.

The 2018 biographical drama **_Bohemian Rhapsody_**, directed by Bryan Singer and starring Rami Malek as Mercury, grossed over **$900 million** on a $52 million budget, shattering the box-office record for a music biopic.

Queen's **_Greatest Hits_** (1981) remains one of the best-selling albums in history, with over **25 million copies** sold in the UK alone — the best-selling album in UK chart history. The band ranks **third all-time among British acts for number-one albums**, behind only The Beatles and The Rolling Stones. Their influence stretches across genres: artists as diverse as Lady Gaga, My Chemical Romance, George Michael, Metallica, and Faith Hill have cited Queen as a formative inspiration.

In 2025, PPL data named Queen the **UK's most played rock act of the 21st century**, having accumulated over **400 million seconds of radio and television airplay** — the equivalent of more than 12.5 continuous years of broadcast — with "A Kind of Magic" their most-aired track. Brian May's response was characteristic: *"Astounding news! Bearing in mind that most of Queen's major works were done in the 20th century, it's amazing to top a 21st century list."*
