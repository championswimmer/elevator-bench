# The Eagles: A Life History

> *"We hadn't broken up. We'd just taken a fourteen-year vacation."*
> — Glenn Frey, on the 1994 reunion

---

## Formation (1971–1972)

The story of the Eagles begins not with the band itself, but with a favour to a friend. In early **1971**, singer **Linda Ronstadt** and her manager **John Boylan** were assembling a touring band to support her sophomore album, *Silk Purse*. Among those recruited were a Texas-born drummer named **Don Henley** — who had moved to Los Angeles with his band Shiloh to record an album produced by Kenny Rogers — and a guitarist from Michigan named **Glenn Frey**, who had arrived in the city with a country-folk duo called Longbranch Pennywhistle. The two had already met in **1970 at the Troubadour** in West Hollywood, a fabled industry watering hole, where both were signed to the small Amos Records label.

Touring together in such close quarters, Frey and Henley discovered that each could sing and write songs with uncommon skill. As Ronstadt later recalled, "Glenn used to call Don his secret weapon. He said, 'I'm gonna do a band with Don. We're gonna do a band together.'" Boylan encouraged them, suggesting veteran bassist **Randy Meisner** — who had previously played with Ricky Nelson's Stone Canyon Band — and Ronstadt herself proposed adding guitarist **Bernie Leadon**, a seasoned player who had come from The Flying Burrito Brothers. The four of them rehearsed in Ronstadt's living room, working out the four-part harmonies that would become their trademark.

Introduced to music mogul **David Geffen** through friend and neighbour **Jackson Browne**, the quartet signed to Geffen's newly formed **Asylum Records** in September 1971. Geffen bought out Frey and Henley's contracts with Amos Records and sent all four to **Aspen, Colorado** to develop as a unit. The band's first documented performance, in October 1971, was under the throw-away name "Teen King and the Emergencies" at a club called The Gallery in Aspen. Their proper name — **Eagles**, without the definite article — reportedly came during a peyote- and tequila-fuelled outing in the **Mojave Desert**, inspired, depending on who's telling the story, by eagles seen soaring overhead or by Bernie Leadon's reading of Hopi mythology.

Their debut album, ***Eagles***, was recorded in England in February 1972 with celebrated producer **Glyn Johns**, and released on **1 June 1972**. Johns shaped their sound into what a *Rolling Stone* article famously called "the country-rock band with those high-flyin' harmonies." The first single, **"Take It Easy"** — co-written by Frey and Jackson Browne, with its iconic opening line set on a corner in Winslow, Arizona — reached number 12 on the *Billboard* Hot 100. It was followed by the bluesy **"Witchy Woman"** (No. 9) and the soft country-rock ballad **"Peaceful Easy Feeling"** (No. 22). The Eagles had arrived.

Their second album, ***Desperado*** (1973), took Old West outlaws as its central metaphor, drawing implicit comparisons between gunslingers and rock stars. Henley and Frey co-wrote eight of its eleven songs, including **"Tequila Sunrise"** and the elegiac title track, both of which became enduring fan favourites despite the album charting only at number 41 on the *Billboard* 200. The sessions marked a decisive shift in creative power: Henley and Frey were now the undisputed core of the Eagles.

## 1970s Success (1973–1980)

For their third album, ***On the Border*** (1974), Henley and Frey wanted a harder-edged sound, prompting the band to replace producer Glyn Johns mid-recording with **Bill Szymczyk**. Looking for an incendiary guitarist for a track called "Good Day in Hell," they remembered a Florida-born slide guitar prodigy named **Don Felder** — nicknamed "Fingers" by Frey after a 1972 backstage jam — and invited him to join as the fifth Eagle in January 1974. *On the Border* yielded the band's **first US number-one single**, "Best of My Love," which hit the top of the charts on 1 March 1975.

The next album, ***One of These Nights*** (June 1975), was a commercial and artistic breakthrough, becoming their **first number-one album** in the US and making them international superstars. The title track became their second chart-topper; **"Lyin' Eyes"** reached number 2 and won the band their first Grammy Award for Best Pop Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal; and **"Take It to the Limit"** — featuring founding bassist Randy Meisner on lead vocals — peaked at number 4. The band appeared on the cover of *Rolling Stone* that September.

But internal tensions were already brewing. Founding member **Bernie Leadon**, increasingly disillusioned with the band's drift away from country music toward rock, famously poured a beer over Glenn Frey's head during a disagreement before departing in **December 1975**. His replacement was guitarist and vocalist **Joe Walsh**, a wild-card hire who had previously fronted the James Gang and recorded as a solo artist. After Walsh's arrival, the Eagles' country roots all but disappeared, replaced by a more complex, arena-ready rock sound.

In early 1976, the Eagles released ***Their Greatest Hits (1971–1975)***, which became the **best-selling album of the twentieth century in the United States**, with over 40 million copies sold domestically. It held the all-time US sales record until Michael Jackson's *Thriller* overtook it following Jackson's death in 2009.

The group then spent a gruelling year and a half recording their fifth studio album, ***Hotel California***, released on **8 December 1976**. A landmark of the rock canon, the album opens with **"New Kid in Town"** (the band's third US number-one) before its centrepiece, the labyrinthine title track: a six-and-a-half-minute meditation on hedonism, disillusion, and the seductive darkness of the American Dream. Don Henley sang lead over an intricate twin-guitar solo by Felder and Walsh, the two guitarists playing off one another in a passage widely regarded as one of the greatest in rock history. Henley later told *60 Minutes*: "It's basically a song about the dark underbelly of the American Dream and about excess in America, which was something we knew about." *Hotel California* won Grammy Awards for Record of the Year and Best Arrangement for Voices, and has sold more than **32 million copies worldwide**, making it the band's best-selling studio album.

Founding bassist **Randy Meisner** departed after the punishing Hotel California world tour, following a backstage confrontation in Knoxville, Tennessee in June 1977 over his reluctance to sing his signature high note on "Take It to the Limit." He was replaced by **Timothy B. Schmit**, who had previously succeeded Meisner in the band Poco — a remarkable piece of rock coincidence.

The Eagles' final studio album of the original era, ***The Long Run*** (September 1979), took a year and a half to record and nearly broke the band. Despite critical disappointment, it topped the charts and sold seven million copies, spawning three Top 10 singles: **"Heartache Tonight"** (their fifth and final US number-one), "The Long Run," and "I Can't Tell You Why."

The end came on **31 July 1980** in Long Beach, California — an event later dubbed the "Long Night at Wrong Beach" — when simmering animosity between Felder and Frey erupted before and during a benefit concert for California Senator Alan Cranston. The two men traded threats across the stage for the entire set. Frey recalled Felder saying, near the end of the show, "Only three more songs until I kick your ass, pal." By the end of 1980, the Eagles had dissolved. Their farewell live album, ***Eagles Live***, was mixed on opposite coasts with Frey refusing to speak to his former bandmates — its liner notes, listing five attorneys, simply read: *"Thank you and goodnight."*

## Breakup and Solo Years (1980–1994)

In the wake of the dissolution, each member struck out on his own. Don Henley had the most critically acclaimed solo career. His 1982 debut, *I Can't Stand Still*, featured the acerbic hit **"Dirty Laundry"**; his 1984 follow-up, *Building the Perfect Beast*, produced the defining late-summer anthem **"The Boys of Summer"** (US No. 5) and **"All She Wants to Do Is Dance"** (No. 9); and his 1989 album *The End of the Innocence*, featuring the title track and **"The Heart of the Matter"**, was another major commercial and artistic success. His solo career was eventually interrupted by a long contract dispute that was only resolved by the band's 1994 reunion.

Glenn Frey also thrived commercially. His 1982 debut *No Fun Aloud* launched "The One You Love" (US No. 15), while the soundtrack work that followed produced his biggest hits: **"The Heat Is On"** from *Beverly Hills Cop* (No. 2) and **"You Belong to the City"** from the *Miami Vice* soundtrack (No. 2), the latter accompanied by Frey's acting appearance in a *Miami Vice* episode titled after his song "Smuggler's Blues."

Joe Walsh had already established himself as a solo artist before joining the Eagles and continued recording and touring throughout the '80s, performing as a session musician for Dan Fogelberg, Steve Winwood, and others, and producing and co-writing Ringo Starr's *Old Wave* album.

Timothy B. Schmit recorded two solo albums, *Playin' It Cool* (1984) and *Tell Me the Truth* (1990), contributed vocals to Crosby, Stills & Nash, spent three years in Jimmy Buffett's Coral Reefer Band, and had a minor Top 40 hit with "Boys' Night Out" in 1987. Don Felder released a solo album and contributed memorable tracks to the *Heavy Metal* film soundtrack.

---

## Reunion (1994–2016)

The catalyst was an unlikely one: a country tribute album. When *Common Thread: The Songs of the Eagles* was released in 1993, country star **Travis Tritt** insisted that the *Long Run*-era Eagles appear in his video for their cover of "Take It Easy." The band agreed — and found themselves on speaking terms again for the first time in over a decade.

On **27 May 1994**, the Eagles — Frey, Henley, Walsh, Felder, and Schmit — took the stage at Irvine Meadows Amphitheatre in California for their **first live performance in fourteen years**. Frey quipped to the crowd: "For the record, we never broke up. We just took a fourteen-year vacation." The reunion was named ***Hell Freezes Over***, invoking Henley's oft-quoted declaration that the band would only get back together "when hell freezes over." The resulting live album — featuring four new studio tracks including **"Get Over It"** and **"Love Will Keep Us Alive"** — debuted at **number one on the *Billboard* chart** and sold six million copies in the US. The accompanying MTV special became one of the most-watched music specials of the year.

The subsequent tour was interrupted when Frey suffered a serious recurrence of diverticulitis in September 1994, but resumed in 1995 and continued into 1996. In **1998, the Eagles were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame**. For the induction ceremony, all seven Eagles — Frey, Henley, Felder, Walsh, Schmit, Leadon, and Meisner — performed together for two songs: "Take It Easy" and "Hotel California." It was a rare gathering of the complete membership.

Don Felder was fired from the Eagles on 6 February 2001, triggering years of legal battles over profits and breach of contract claims; the case was settled out of court in 2007. The band carried on as a quartet — Frey, Henley, Walsh, and Schmit — adding guitarist Steuart Smith to fill Felder's role.

In **October 2007**, the Eagles released ***Long Road Out of Eden***, their first album of entirely new material since *The Long Run*, 28 years earlier. It debuted at **number one in six countries** and won a Grammy for Best Country Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal ("How Long"). Henley told CNN at the time: "This is probably the last Eagles album we'll ever make." The supporting Long Road Out of Eden Tour ran from 2008 to 2011.

In **2013**, the band released the career-spanning documentary ***History of the Eagles*** and launched an extended arena tour with founding guitarist Bernie Leadon rejoining for the performances. The History of the Eagles Tour continued through July 2015 and was among the highest-grossing tours of its era.

On **18 January 2016**, founding member and co-leader **Glenn Frey died** at Columbia University Medical Center in New York City, aged 67, from complications involving rheumatoid arthritis, ulcerative colitis, and pneumonia. At the 58th Grammy Awards a month later, the surviving Eagles — joined by Leadon, touring guitarist Steuart Smith, and Jackson Browne — performed "Take It Easy" in his honour. Henley publicly stated he did not think the band would ever perform again.

---

## Legacy (2017–Present)

Despite Henley's grief-stricken words, the Eagles returned. In **July 2017**, at the Classic West and Classic East concert festivals organised by longtime manager Irving Azoff, the band performed with **Vince Gill** and **Deacon Frey** — Glenn's son — sharing Frey's vocals and guitar parts. The shows were a testament to the enduring draw of the Eagles' catalogue and a moving tribute to their fallen co-founder.

The band continued to tour through 2018, 2019, and beyond, performing *Hotel California* in its entirety with a 46-piece orchestra and 22-voice choir during a celebrated 2019 Las Vegas residency. After the Covid-19 pandemic delayed plans in 2020, the Hotel California Tour resumed and expanded into 2021 and 2022. Deacon Frey departed to pursue a solo career in April 2022 but later returned to join the band's announced **farewell tour, "The Long Goodbye,"** which launched at Madison Square Garden on 7 September 2023.

Founding bassist **Randy Meisner** died on 26 July 2023, aged 77, from complications of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, leaving Henley and Leadon as the two surviving original members. In **September 2024**, the Eagles began an ambitious concert residency at the **Sphere** in Las Vegas — one of the most technologically sophisticated concert venues ever built — with the first eight shows grossing over $42 million from 131,000 tickets sold. As of early 2026, Don Henley has indicated the Eagles will disband at the end of their current touring cycle: "The time has come for us to close the circle."

The Eagles' place in music history is beyond dispute. They hold **six Grammy Awards**, were ranked **number 75** on *Rolling Stone*'s 100 Greatest Artists of All Time, and have sold more than **200 million records worldwide** — including over 100 million in the United States alone. Their 1976 compilation *Their Greatest Hits (1971–1975)* remains the **best-selling album in US history**, certified Quadruple Diamond (40 million copies) by the RIAA in 2026. *Hotel California* stands at number 37 on *Rolling Stone*'s list of the 500 Greatest Albums of All Time.

Beyond the chart statistics, the Eagles' influence runs through the DNA of American popular music. Their mastery of close-harmony singing, their fusion of country grit and rock ambition, the cinematic sweep of their storytelling — from "Desperado" to "The Last Resort" — set a standard for craft in arena rock that few have matched. As Don Henley once reflected: "We were writing about the California Dream — and the reality behind it. That's why those songs still resonate. The dream hasn't changed."
